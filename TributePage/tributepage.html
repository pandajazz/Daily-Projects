MILES DAVIS

A Smiley Miles Davis

Miles Dewey Davis III (May 26, 1926 / September 28, 1991) was an American jazz trumpeter, bandleader, and composer.
He is among the most influential and acclaimed figures in the history of jazz and 20th century music.
Davis adopted a variety of musical directions in a five-decade career that kept him at the forefront of many major stylistic developments in jazz.

  1. 1944 - made his professional debut as a member of saxophonist Charlie Parker's bebop quintet
  2. 50s - recorded some of the earliest hard bop music while on Prestige Records
  3. 1955 - signed a long-term contract with Columbia Records and recorded 'Round About Midnight (1957), Sketches of Spain (1960), and band recordings, such as Milestones (1958) and Kind of Blue (1959)
  4. early 60s - made several line-up changes while recording Someday My Prince Will Come, Blackhawk concerts (1961) and Seven Steps to Heaven (1963)
  5. late 60s - with his new quintet he led on a series of more abstract recordings, helping pioneer the post-bop genre with albums such as E.S.P (1965) and Miles Smiles (1967), before transitioning into his electric period
  6. 70s - he experimented with rock, funk, African rhythms, emerging electronic music technology, and an ever-changing line-up of musicians. This period, includind In a Silent Way (1969), Bitches Brew (1970) and Agharta (1975) was the most controversial in his career
  7. 80s - after a five-year retirement, he resumed his career: employing younger musicians and pop sounds on albums such as Man with the Horn (1981) and Tutu (1986). Critics were generally unreceptive but the decade garnered the trumpeter his highest level of commercial recognition
  8. September 28, 1991 - Davis died for the combined effects of a stroke, pneumonia, and respiratory failure
  9. 2006 - Davis was inducted into the Rock and Roll Hall of Fame, which recognized him as "one of the key figures in the history of jazz."
